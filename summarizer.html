Finding the Best Delivery Routes Using Bing Maps API

Starting at the green marker, what do you think is the most efficient way to hit all the blue markers and then go back to green? Zoom/move around!

Make this Notebook Trusted to load map: File -> Trust Notebook

Background

A close friend of mine is the owner of a local Miami-based business called breakfastfiesta which started seeing a lot of success around 2019. The demand can be a little overwhelming on days like Valentine's and Mother's Day. The blue points above were all the orders we had to deliver on Mother's Day 2020, and the green point was where we were delivering from. We didn't use any strategy that day besides eyeballing groups of close addresses and getting to them on the same trip. But even that took more than 12 hours to do with 2 cars. We wanted to come up with a better solution going forward. This project is a backtest of that day, in order to come up with a solution that she can use for future Valentine's and Mother's Days. This was also around the time that I saw the traveling salesman problem in class.

Data Preprocessing

Before doing anything we had to standardize our clients' addresses to make it easy to convert to latitude and longitude using geocod.io. We also needed to separate all pickup orders; we only want to deal with deliveries here. Creating this Excel file that took care of all that was an important first step.

Algorithm of the Ideal Model

I did a variation of the Nearest Neighbor algorithm, a naive heuristic solution to the TSP.

High level overview of the algorithm

  1. Start at my friend's house
  2. Calculate the times between start point and all unvisited delivery spots
  3. Go to the delivery spot with the shortest time to get there
  4. Make that delivery spot the new start point
  5. Repeat 2-4 until no more points

A More Realistic Model

The ideal model gets to all 51 deliveries in a row without ever stopping to get more. A more realistic model would go back home to resupply after making n deliveries and continue. The 2nd version of the program takes resupplying into account. How long it waits to go home depends on a variable called space. In the following case, space has an value of 6. Six is how many deliveries my car could hold, so every 6 I had to go back to her house and get more.

The output for a more realistic version: a car that has space for 6 deliveries. I highlighted every time it goes home.

1st trip: It starts off on the exact same route as the ideal model, except that once it's delivered as many as it can (whatever the variable space is equal to) it goes back home.

2nd trip: Instead of continuing where the ideal model left off, it calculates a new closest point from the house (in this case B) until it runs out of space again, and repeats. Doing it this way is ~23 minutes faster than the ideal route with the same # of stops inbetween.

Delivery Time as a Function of Vehicle Space

Bottom to top. +1 space at low spaces decreases delivery time by a lot. There are diminishing returns and sometimes even worse times at higher spaces.

I wanted to know: if I had a car that could hold 1, another that could hold 2, 3, 4, ... , all the way to 51 (the ideal model), how would they compare in time? I ran the program for all possible spaces to get this dataset.

So if 6 is my car, then 1 is a bicycle and maybe 51 is a cargo truck (assuming same speed). Looking at the bottom of the pic, an improvement from a capacity of 6 to 15 saves 20.68% of the total time (about 2 hours and 40 min). By messing around with it you can see 6 to 10 saves ~1h30m, 10 to 15 saves ~1h15m, 1 to 2 saves almost 17 hours, 50 to 51 saves an hour and a half. 9 to 10 loses you an hour and a half surprisingly.

There's a lot of those un-improvements in there, shown by the graph sticking out to the right. That's probably because if space = 10 for example, then it has to do 10 per run, even if in a particular instance 7 or 3 or 9 would be better. At the end, it has to dedicate a whole trip to the farthest person's address, since it already did 5 trips of 10. That leaves a lot up to chance--the divisibility of numbers--instead of computational decision-making.

If I were to implement some sort of AI to pick its own delivery routes and sizes, the sums of the right side of that graph could be a good measure of its effectiveness.

The Time Value of Money

I looked into how much Home Depot charges for van and truck rentals, how long it would take to complete the route with each of them, and did some quick maths on a Google sheet to see whether the cost increase was worth the time save. 


Paying by the day:
1 van saves 2.81 hours but costs $129.
1 truck saves 4.54 hours but costs $139.

Paying by the hour:
1 van saves 2.81 hours but costs $171.91
1 truck saves 4.54 hours but costs $222.23
2 vans save 8.52 hours but cost $340.91
2 trucks save 9.52 hours but cost $426.96	

Paying 1 truck by the day is a good compromise between cost and time.

2 vans is also not a bad idea, especially if she can squeeze in some big orders near her house.

Final Thoughts

The project is not yet perfect or even complete. My method for modeling multiple cars right now is just splitting the list up, which is not ideal. I would've loved to have an animation that shows the car traversing all the points as the program runs. There are also more thorough algorithms out there for the problem, like Branch and Bound. If I were to do something similar again, I'd use a more complex method and see what I can learn. I like where the project stands as for right now though.

This project was a proper intro to programming for me. I had a lot of fun figuring things out and debugging things. I learned to think more programmatically and to think ahead. Most importantly, I was able to give my friend some recordkeeping and decision-making tools that will hopefully help her processes in the future.
